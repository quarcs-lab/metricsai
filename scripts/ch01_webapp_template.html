§ 1.1 · Key Concept 1.1

Data at a glance — descriptive statistics

Before running any regression, understand the raw data. Pick a variable from the 29 houses in Central Davis, CA and scan its centre, spread, and shape.

Try this
  1. Select Sale price. Mean = $253,910, median = $244,000. The mean sits above the median — what does that tell you about the tail?
  2. Switch to Size. The range is 1,400–3,300 sq ft. Std dev is $398. Are houses more or less variable in size than in price?
  3. Switch to Bedrooms. The distribution is discrete — 3, 4, 5, or 6. How does that affect the histogram?
§ 1.3 – § 1.4 · Key Concepts 1.3 & 1.4

Scatter plot & regression line — seeing the relationship

A scatter plot reveals the direction, form, and strength of a relationship before any numbers are calculated. Toggle the fitted regression line and the residuals to see how the OLS line summarizes the cloud of points.

Try this
  1. Turn the regression line off. Can you mentally draw a line through the cloud? Where would you put it?
  2. Turn it back on. The OLS line minimizes the sum of squared residuals — not eye-balled, computed.
  3. Toggle residuals on. Find the house with the tallest vertical bar — it's the worst-predicted house. What might explain the gap? (Condition? Age? Location?)
§ 1.5 · Key Concepts 1.5 & 1.9

Prediction explorer — what does the slope mean?

The slope coefficient says: "for each additional square foot, the predicted price increases by $73.77." Drag the size slider to see predictions move along the fitted line. Then experiment with the what-if slope slider to feel how coefficient uncertainty translates into prediction uncertainty.

Try this
  1. Set size to 2,000 sq ft (the chapter's example). Predicted price should be ~$262,559. Does it match?
  2. Increase by 100 sq ft. The price goes up by exactly $slope × 100. That is the marginal effect.
  3. Drag size below 1,400 or above 3,300 (the data range). You are now extrapolating — the line may not hold there.
  4. Drag the slope slider to $60/sqft, then to $90. For a 2,000 sq ft house, the prediction range is ~$40,000 wide. That is estimation uncertainty in action.
§ 1.5 · Key Concept 1.5

R² — how much variation does the regression explain?

R² decomposes the total variation in price: how much is "explained" by the regression line (variation of predicted values) versus "unexplained" (residual scatter). Toggle the view to see both components on the scatter plot.

Try this
  1. Click Explained. The vertical cyan segments show how far each prediction deviates from the mean price. This is the variation the model "explains."
  2. Click Residual. The pink segments show how far each actual price deviates from the prediction. This is the variation left over.
  3. R² = 0.6175 means 61.75% is explained. Is that good? For 29 houses with one predictor, it is respectable — but 38% is still unexplained.
§ 1.6 · Key Concept 1.6

Multiple predictors — association is not causation

Pick any of the five available predictors and see how the regression changes. Each produces a different slope and R² — but none of them prove that the predictor causes the price. Omitted variables (condition, location, school district) could be driving both.

Try this
  1. Start with Size (R² ≈ 0.62). Switch to Bedrooms. R² drops. Larger houses have more bedrooms — could bedrooms just be a proxy for size?
  2. Try Age. Is the slope positive or negative? What does that tell you about newer vs. older homes in this neighbourhood?
  3. Five regressions, five different fits. If size "explains" 62% but age explains much less, does that mean size causes higher prices? No — an omitted variable like location could confound both.
