§ 13.2 · Key Concepts 13.2 & 13.3

Cobb-Douglas production function

The log-linear Cobb-Douglas model estimates capital and labor elasticities: ln(Q) = β₁ + α·ln(K) + β·ln(L). If α + β = 1, the technology exhibits constant returns to scale.

Try this
  1. Capital elasticity α = 0.23: a 1% increase in capital raises output by 0.23%. Labor elasticity β = 0.81: labor is the dominant input.
  2. α + β = 1.04 ≈ 1. The F-test for constant returns to scale gives p = 0.61 — we cannot reject CRS.
  3. The predicted output (purple) tracks actual output (cyan) closely. R² = 0.96 — an excellent fit for 24 years of data.
§ 13.3 · Key Concepts 13.4 & 13.5

Phillips curve & omitted variables bias

The Phillips curve — the negative relationship between unemployment and inflation — broke down after 1970. The culprit: omitted expected inflation. When expectations are controlled for, the negative relationship reappears.

Try this
  1. Pre-1970: the classic negative slope (−1.03). Higher unemployment → lower inflation.
  2. Switch to post-1970: the slope flips to +0.27! This is the breakdown that shocked economists.
  3. The OVB formula explains it: E[b₂] = β₂ + β₃γ = −0.13 + 1.15 × 0.34 = +0.26 ≈ actual +0.27. Perfect match.
  4. The augmented model (controlling for expected inflation) recovers the negative effect: −0.13.
§ 13.5 · Key Concept 13.7

RAND Health Insurance Experiment (RCT)

The RAND experiment randomly assigned families to insurance plans with different cost-sharing. Because assignment was random, spending differences across plans are causal estimates of moral hazard.

Try this
  1. Free care: $2,154/year. 95% cost-sharing: $1,046/year. People spend 51% less when they pay out of pocket — moral hazard confirmed.
  2. The F-test (F = 11.39, p < 0.001) confirms that plan assignment matters. This is a joint test that all plan coefficients equal zero.
  3. R² = 0.007 is intentionally low. Most variation in medical spending is random health shocks, not insurance plan — the experiment detects the signal anyway.
§ 13.6 · Key Concept 13.8

Difference-in-Differences: health clinic access

South African children in communities that gained health clinics (treatment) improved more than children in communities without clinics (control). The DiD design controls for pre-existing differences and common time trends.

Try this
  1. Both groups improved from 1993 to 1998 (common trend). But treated communities improved more.
  2. DiD = (treated change) − (control change) = 0.52 standard deviations. That's like moving from the 25th to the 45th percentile in weight.
  3. The 95% CI is [0.06, 0.98] — it just excludes zero (p = 0.027). The effect is significant but the interval is wide due to clustering.
§ 13.7 · Key Concept 13.9

Regression discontinuity: incumbency advantage

Candidates who barely win a Senate race (margin > 0) gain an incumbency advantage in the next election. At the threshold, winning is quasi-random — creating a natural experiment.

Try this
  1. The jump at margin = 0 is the incumbency advantage: 4.8 percentage points. Barely winning → nearly 5% more votes next time.
  2. Toggle to "Raw scatter" to see the full data. The jump is visible even without binning.
  3. The 95% CI is [3.1, 6.5] — clearly excludes zero (p < 0.001). This is one of the most robust findings in political science.
  4. Note the linear slopes on each side. The slope (0.35) means that a 10-point wider margin adds 3.5% to the next election vote.
§ 13.8 · Key Concept 13.10

Instrumental variables: institutions and GDP

Do institutions cause growth, or does growth improve institutions? OLS can't tell. Using historical settler mortality as an instrument — it affects institutions but not modern GDP directly — IV estimates the causal effect.

Try this
  1. OLS: institutions coefficient = 0.52. But this is biased by reverse causation and omitted variables.
  2. First stage: settler mortality strongly predicts institutions (F = 16.3 > 10). The instrument is relevant.
  3. IV/2SLS: institutions coefficient = 0.94 — nearly twice the OLS estimate. OLS was biased downward (attenuation bias).
  4. Interpretation: a 1-unit improvement in institutions → 156% increase in GDP (e^0.94 ≈ 2.56).
