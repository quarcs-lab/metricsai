Key Concept 14.1: Regressing earnings on a gender indicator is equivalent to a difference-in-means test. The intercept equals the male mean ($63,476) and the gender coefficient equals the gap (−$16,396). Adding controls reveals how much of the raw gap is explained by observable differences.
Try This
  1. Start with Model 1 (gender only). The gap is −$16,396 — the raw difference in means. R² is only 2.5% — gender alone explains very little of earnings variation.
  2. Switch to Model 2 (+ education). The gap changes to −$18,258. It increased! Why? Because women have slightly more education on average, so controlling for education makes the residual gap larger.
  3. Switch to Model 4 (full controls). How much of the original gap remains after controlling for education, age, and hours?

Key Concept 14.3: An interaction term Gender×Education allows the slope of education to differ by gender. Without it, the model forces parallel regression lines. The interaction coefficient measures how much the female slope differs from the male slope.
Try This
  1. With interaction OFF, both genders share the same slope ($5,907/year). The lines are perfectly parallel — only the intercept differs.
  2. Toggle interaction ON. Now males get $6,921/year and females get $6,921 − $2,765 = $4,156/year. Are the lines still parallel?
  3. Follow the lines to high education (20 years). Is the gender gap larger or smaller at higher education? What does this mean economically?

Key Concept 14.4: When a model includes both an indicator and its interactions, individual t-tests on each piece may not be significant — but the set of gender-related terms may be jointly significant. Always use an F-test for joint significance.
Try This
  1. Compare the gender row across all 5 models. The coefficient changes sign from Model 1 to Model 3! Why? (Hint: the interaction term absorbs part of the effect.)
  2. Look at R². How much does each additional variable contribute? Is the jump from Model 1 to Model 2 (adding education) larger than Model 4 to Model 5?
  3. In Model 5, the gender coefficient is $57,129. Does this mean women earn more? No — you must evaluate the total gender effect at specific values of education, age, and hours.

Key Concept 14.6: Including all k indicators plus an intercept creates perfect multicollinearity (the dummy variable trap). Drop one category to make the model estimable. The coefficients on the remaining indicators measure differences from the base.
Try This
  1. With Private as base, the coefficients on dself and dgovt show differences from the private sector mean.
  2. Switch to Self-employed as base. The coefficients change completely — but look at the predicted means (bottom row). They are identical.
  3. Switch to Government. Same R², same predictions. Only the interpretation changes: each coefficient now measures the difference from government workers.
§14.5

Worker Type Comparison

Mean annual earnings by employment type, with 95% confidence intervals. These group means are exactly what the indicator-variable regression recovers.

Key Concept 14.7: ANOVA (analysis of variance) is equivalent to regressing the outcome on a set of indicator variables. The F-test for joint significance of the indicators tests whether all group means are equal.
Try This
  1. Which job type has the highest mean earnings? Note the wide confidence interval for self-employed — the small sample (n=79) and high variance make the estimate less precise.
  2. Do the confidence intervals for private and government overlap? If so, the difference may not be statistically significant.
  3. The self-employed mean ($72,306) looks highest, but its CI is very wide. A few high earners pull it up — this is common with self-employment income.

Key Concept 14.8: Plotting separate regression lines by group is the visual counterpart of an interaction model. If the lines are not parallel, returns to education differ by gender — and the gender gap changes across the education distribution.
Try This
  1. Look at both regression lines. Are they parallel? If not, the interaction term (Gender×Education) is needed in the model.
  2. Compare the gap at 12 years vs 16 years of education. Does the gap grow or shrink with more education?
  3. Toggle to Male only, then Female only. Notice the different spreads — male earnings have more outliers at the top.
