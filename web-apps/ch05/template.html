§ 5.1 · Key Concept 5.1

Bivariate summary statistics

Before examining the relationship between two variables, summarize each one separately. Pick a variable pair and compare means, medians, spreads, and coefficients of variation side by side.

Try this
  1. Compare CVs for price vs size: size (CV = 0.21) is more variable than price (CV = 0.15) relative to their means.
  2. Switch to price vs age. The covariance is near zero — these two variables barely co-move.
  3. Check bedrooms: the mean (3.79) is close to the median (4) — little skewness in this discrete variable.
§ 5.3 – § 5.4 · Key Concepts 5.3 & 5.4

Scatterplot & correlation coefficient

A scatterplot reveals direction, strength, form, and outliers. The correlation coefficient r summarizes the linear strength in a single number between −1 and +1. Pick an X variable and watch r change.

Try this
  1. Price vs size: r = 0.79 (strong positive) — the points form a clear upward band.
  2. Switch to bedrooms: r = 0.43 (moderate) — the scatter is much looser. Is this still a useful predictor?
  3. Switch to age: r = −0.07 (near zero) — the cloud is nearly random. Size matters for price; age barely does.
  4. For any pair, hover over the most distant point from the trend. Is it an outlier, or just natural variation in n = 29?
§ 5.4 · Key Concept 5.4

What does a given correlation look like?

Correlation coefficients are abstract until you see the scatter. Drag the slider to visualize synthetic data at different target r values — from perfect negative (−1) through zero to perfect positive (+1).

Try this
  1. Set r to 0.8 (close to the house data's 0.79). Notice how the points form a clear upward band.
  2. Slide to r = 0.0. The cloud has no direction — you cannot predict Y from X.
  3. Slide to r = −0.5. The downward trend is visible but loose — compare this to 0.8's tightness.
  4. Slide to r = 1.0. All points fall on a perfect line — and any real dataset will look looser than this.
§ 5.5 – § 5.6 · Key Concepts 5.5 & 5.6

OLS regression line

Ordinary Least Squares chooses the line that minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances (residuals) from each point to the line. Pick a predictor, toggle residuals on, and slide to predict at any X value.

Try this
  1. With size as X, read the equation: price = $115,017 + $73.77 × size. Move the prediction slider to 2,000 sqft — the predicted price is $262,559.
  2. Toggle "Show residuals" on. The vertical lines from each point to the line are the residuals. OLS chose the line that makes the sum of squared lengths as small as possible.
  3. Switch X to bedrooms. The slope is steeper ($23,667 per bedroom) but R² drops to 0.18 — the relationship is real but noisy.
  4. Switch X to age. The slope is near zero and R² = 0.005 — age tells us almost nothing about price.
§ 5.6 · Key Concept 5.6

R-squared decomposition — where does the variation go?

Total variation (TSS) splits into explained variation (ESS) and residual variation (RSS). R² = ESS / TSS tells you the fraction the model explains. Toggle between the three views to see each component on the scatter.

Try this
  1. In "All" view, see the three-number split: ESS (cyan) = 61.7% of TSS. That percentage is R².
  2. Switch to "Total." The purple lines show how far each house price is from the average ($253,910). This is the variation the model tries to explain.
  3. Switch to "Explained." The cyan lines show how far each predicted price is from the average — variation captured by the model.
  4. Switch to "Residual." The pink lines show prediction errors. Smaller = better fit.
§ 5.7 · Key Concept 5.7

Regression asymmetry — Y~X ≠ X~Y

If regression measured a symmetric, causal relationship, then regressing price on size and size on price should give reciprocal slopes. They don't — proving that regression captures association, not causation.

Try this
  1. Start with "price ~ size." The slope is $73.77 per sqft. If regression were symmetric, regressing size on price should give 1/73.77 = 0.01356 sqft per dollar.
  2. Switch to "size ~ price." The actual slope is 0.00837 — NOT 0.01356. These are different questions.
  3. Toggle to "Both." The two lines cross at the mean point (1,883 sqft, $253,910) but diverge everywhere else.
  4. Notice R² is the same in both directions (0.6175). Correlation is symmetric; regression is not.
§ 5.11

Parametric vs nonparametric regression

OLS assumes a straight line. LOWESS lets the data speak — it fits local regressions at each point, with the bandwidth controlling how "local." When LOWESS and OLS agree, the linearity assumption is validated.

Try this
  1. With OLS and LOWESS both on (frac = 0.65), notice they trace nearly the same path. The linear assumption holds.
  2. Drag the bandwidth down to 0.30. LOWESS becomes wiggly — overfitting 29 data points.
  3. Push bandwidth to 1.00. LOWESS is nearly as straight as OLS. When the flexible method agrees with the rigid one, parsimony wins.
  4. Turn on kernel smoothing. It tells the same story: the price–size relationship is genuinely linear.
