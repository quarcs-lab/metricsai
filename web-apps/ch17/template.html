§ 17.2 · Key Concept 17.1

Panel data variance decomposition

Panel data has two sources of variation: between (differences across individuals in their averages) and within (deviations from each individual's average over time). Pooled OLS uses both; fixed effects uses only within.

Try this
  1. Log revenue: between SD (0.21) is twice the within SD (0.11). Most variation in NBA revenue is across teams, not over time.
  2. Wins: between SD and within SD are closer. Team performance fluctuates substantially from season to season.
  3. Fixed effects uses only the within bars. If within variation is small, FE estimates are less precise.
§ 17.2 · Key Concept 17.2

Standard error comparison — why clustering matters

In panel data, observations within the same team are correlated over time. Default SEs ignore this, producing artificially small standard errors. Cluster-robust SEs account for within-team correlation.

Try this
  1. The cluster SE is 1.81× larger than the default. That's enough to change the conclusion about statistical significance.
  2. The robust (HC1) SE is only 1.15× larger — it corrects for heteroskedasticity but not for within-team correlation.
  3. With cluster SEs, the wins coefficient is still significant (p = 0.0004). But the confidence interval is almost twice as wide.
§ 17.3 · Key Concepts 17.3 & 17.4

Pooled OLS vs fixed effects

Pooled OLS treats all 286 observations as independent. Fixed effects adds team-specific intercepts, removing the between-team variation that could confound the wins–revenue relationship.

Try this
  1. Pooled OLS: wins coef = 0.0068 — an extra win is associated with 0.68% higher revenue. But this mixes between and within variation.
  2. Switch to FE: wins coef = 0.0045 — smaller. FE removes confounding from persistent team characteristics (market size, arena, brand).
  3. Toggle "Both": the FE line is flatter. Pooled OLS overstates the effect because big-market teams win more AND earn more.
  4. FE R² (within) = 0.19. Only 19% of within-team revenue variation is explained by wins alone.
§ 17.5 · Key Concept 17.5

Interest rates — levels vs changes

Levels of interest rates trend together (non-stationary). First differences remove the trend, producing stationary series that fluctuate around zero. Regressing non-stationary levels produces a spurious R².

Try this
  1. Levels: both rates trend downward from ~14% in 1982 to ~2% by 2015. This shared trend inflates R².
  2. Switch to Changes: the series fluctuate around zero with no visible trend — this is stationarity.
  3. The amplitude of monthly changes is small (±1 pp) compared to the 12-point range in levels.
§ 17.6 · Key Concept 17.6

Autocorrelation — the smoking gun of non-stationarity

A correlogram (ACF plot) reveals how strongly residuals at lag k correlate with the current residual. Slowly decaying bars are the signature of non-stationarity; bars dropping quickly to zero indicate a well-specified model.

Try this
  1. Levels: ACF at lag 1 = 0.98, at lag 10 still above 0.80. These residuals are highly persistent — the levels regression is spurious.
  2. Switch to Changes: ACF drops to 0.25 at lag 1 and mostly stays within the confidence bands after lag 3.
  3. Switch to ADL(2,2): ACF at lag 1 = 0.02 — virtually zero. The autoregressive terms have absorbed the serial correlation.
§ 17.5 – § 17.7 · Key Concepts 17.5 & 17.7

Spurious regression — R² that lies

Regressing 10-year on 1-year rates in levels gives R² = 0.91 — impressive but misleading. The same regression in changes gives R² = 0.57. The difference is the spurious correlation from shared trends.

Try this
  1. Levels: R² = 0.91, slope = 0.84. But the residuals are highly autocorrelated (ACF1 = 0.98) — the R² is inflated.
  2. Switch to Changes: R² = 0.57, slope = 0.72. Lower but honest — the residual autocorrelation drops to 0.25.
  3. The levels SE (default) is 0.013 — absurdly small. HAC SE is 0.045 (3.4× larger). Default SEs are untrustworthy with non-stationary data.
  4. The changes slope (0.72) is a more reliable estimate of how monthly rate movements co-move.
§ 17.6 – § 17.7 · Key Concept 17.7

ADL(2,2) model — dynamic multipliers

An autoregressive distributed lag model adds own lags and predictor lags. The impact multiplier is the contemporaneous effect; cumulative multipliers show how the total effect builds over time.

Try this
  1. The impact multiplier (γ₀ = 0.86) means a 1 pp change in the 1-year rate immediately moves the 10-year rate by 0.86 pp.
  2. After 1 month, the cumulative effect is 0.31 (γ₀ + γ₁) — actually less because γ₁ is negative (partial reversal).
  3. After 2 months, it recovers to 0.54 as γ₂ is positive again. Interest rate transmission is not monotonic.
  4. The ADL(2,2) eliminates residual autocorrelation (ACF1 = 0.02) — a well-specified dynamic model.
