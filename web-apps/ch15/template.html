Key Concept 15.1: In a log-linear model (ln y = β0 + β1x), the coefficient β1 is approximately the percentage change in y for a one-unit increase in x. In a levels model (y = β0 + β1x), the coefficient is the dollar change.
Try This
  1. In Levels, each year of education adds ~$5,737 to earnings. In Log-linear, it adds ~10%. Which is more useful for comparing across income levels?
  2. Compare R²: the log model (0.176) fits better than levels (0.103). Why? Because earnings have a right-skewed distribution, and logs compress it.
  3. Look at the scatter: in levels, the fitted line badly misses high earners. In logs, the fit is more even across the range.

Quadratic Model & Turning Point

The relationship between age and earnings is not linear — it rises, peaks, and then declines. A quadratic model captures this inverted-U shape and identifies the peak age.

Key Concept 15.3: The turning point x* = −β1 / (2β2) is where the marginal effect changes sign. Below x*, additional age increases earnings. Above x*, earnings decline. The marginal effect at any age is ME = β1 + 2β2 × age.
Try This

  2. Slide age to the turning point (~49.5 years). The marginal effect should be approximately $0 — one more year of age neither helps nor hurts.
  3. Slide age to 60. The marginal effect is negative: earnings decrease with additional age beyond the peak.
§15.5

Standardized Coefficients

Raw coefficients have different units (years, 0/1, log-hours). Standardized coefficients (β* = β × sx/sy) measure the effect in standard deviations — making variables comparable.

Key Concept 15.5: A standardized coefficient of 0.40 means a one-standard-deviation increase in x is associated with a 0.40 SD increase in y. This lets you compare the relative importance of education (years) vs. gender (0/1) vs. hours (log) on the same scale.
Try This
  1. In Raw mode, lnhours has the largest coefficient (0.94) and gender has −0.19. But these numbers are not comparable because the variables have different scales.
  2. Switch to Standardized. Now education (β* = 0.40) is the strongest predictor. lnhours drops to 0.23 because hours have low variance in this sample.
  3. Gender (β* = −0.13) looks small in standardized units. Does this mean the gender gap is unimportant? Not necessarily — a 13% of a standard deviation is still ~$7,000.

Key Concept 15.6: In earnings = β0 + β1educ + β2age + β3(educ × age), the marginal effect of education is ME = β1 + β3 × age. This means the return to education increases with experience.
Try This
  1. At age 25, each year of education adds ~$5,241. At age 55, it adds ~$6,112. The interaction term ($29/year of age) means education pays off more for experienced workers.
  2. The marginal effect line slopes upward. This makes economic sense: education and experience are complementary — more education is worth more when combined with more experience.
  3. Is the interaction economically large? From age 25 to 55, the return increases by ~$871/year ($29 × 30). That is a modest but meaningful difference.

Retransformation Bias

When you predict in logs and convert back with exp(), you systematically underpredict. The smearing correction factor exp(σ²/2) fixes this — compare naive vs. corrected predictions.

Key Concept 15.7: Jensen’s inequality means E[exp(ln ŷ)] ≠ exp(E[ln ŷ]). The naive retransformation exp(ln ŷ) underpredicts the mean by a factor of exp(σ²/2). For this model, the smearing factor is ~1.18, meaning naive predictions are ~18% too low.
Try This
  1. Look at the Compare both view. The naive bar underpredicts the actual mean by ~$9,500 (18%). The corrected bar is much closer.
  2. The smearing factor is exp(0.34/2) ≈ 1.18. This means every naive prediction must be multiplied by 1.18 to remove the downward bias.
  3. Even after correction, the predicted mean ($55,501) does not exactly equal the actual ($56,369). Why? The smearing correction assumes normally distributed errors, which is approximate.
§15.2

Residual Distribution: Levels vs. Log

One reason to use log transformations: the residuals from a levels model are right-skewed, while those from a log model are more symmetric — closer to the normality assumption.

Key Concept 15.2: Choosing between levels and logs is partly about which specification produces better-behaved residuals. Right-skewed residuals suggest that a log transformation may improve the model. The skewness statistic quantifies this: closer to 0 is better.
Try This
  1. Look at the Levels residuals. The histogram has a long right tail — positive residuals (underpredictions) are much larger than negative ones.
  2. Switch to Log residuals. The distribution is much more symmetric. The skewness value drops substantially.
  3. This is why economists often use ln(earnings) rather than earnings: it produces residuals that are closer to the normal distribution assumed by OLS inference.
