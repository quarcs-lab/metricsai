Key Concept 16.1: The Variance Inflation Factor quantifies multicollinearity: VIFj = 1/(1 - Rj²). VIF = 1 means no collinearity; VIF > 10 indicates serious problems (SEs inflated by √10 ≈ 3.2×). While OLS remains unbiased, individual coefficients become imprecise. Joint F-tests remain powerful despite multicollinearity.
Try this
  1. Start with the Base model -- VIF values near 1.0 indicate no multicollinearity. Now toggle to + Interaction and watch VIF jump to 22-37.
  2. Drag the threshold slider to 5. Even the base model variables stay below threshold, but with interaction, all three exceed it.
  3. Note the correlation heatmap: age×education correlates 0.73 with age and 0.64 with education -- these high correlations drive the VIF.

Key Concept 16.4: Heteroskedasticity means error variance depends on regressors: Var[ui|xi] ≠ σ². OLS coefficients remain unbiased, but default SEs are wrong -- typically too small, giving false confidence. Use HC1 robust SEs, which are valid whether or not heteroskedasticity is present. Always use robust SEs for cross-sectional data.
Try this
  1. In SE Comparison mode, compare bar heights. Robust SEs are consistently larger -- the ratio for education is about 1.12.
  2. Toggle to Residual Scatter. Look for the fan shape -- residuals spread wider at higher fitted values, indicating heteroskedasticity.
  3. Focus on Education Only to see that its SE inflates more than age's, because education has more heteroskedastic patterns in earnings.

Key Concept 16.5: Autocorrelation means errors are correlated over time (Cov[ut, us] ≠ 0). OLS remains unbiased but SEs are wrong -- typically too small, leading to false significance. Use HAC (Newey-West) SEs for valid inference. The ACF of residuals reveals the problem: significant bars at multiple lags indicate autocorrelation.
Try this
  1. Set ρ = 0. The time series looks like white noise and ACF bars are within the confidence band. Now drag to ρ = 0.80 -- errors cluster in long runs.
  2. At ρ = 0.95, notice the ACF decays very slowly -- lag 10 is still large. The effective sample size drops dramatically (shown in the stat box).
  3. Click Resimulate at ρ = 0.80 several times. Each realization looks different, but the slow ACF decay pattern is consistent.

Key Concept 16.7: Diagnostic plots assess model adequacy: (1) actual vs. fitted shows how well the model predicts -- points should cluster around the 45-degree line; (2) residual vs. fitted detects heteroskedasticity (fan shape) and nonlinearity (curved pattern). Random scatter around zero with constant spread indicates assumptions are satisfied.
Try this

  2. Toggle to Residual vs. Fitted. Look for fan shapes or curvature. With controls, residuals look more randomly scattered.
  3. Turn on Show Countries to identify which nations are outliers. Hover over extreme points to see country names.

Influential Observations

DFITS measures how much each observation influences its own prediction. Points exceeding the threshold 2√(k/n) warrant investigation. Toggle to DFBETAS to see influence on the growth coefficient specifically.

Key Concept 16.8: DFITS measures influence on fitted values: DFITSi is the scaled change in ŷi when observation i is excluded. DFBETAS measures influence on individual coefficients. Thresholds: |DFITS| > 2√(k/n) and |DFBETAS| > 2/√n. Don't automatically delete influential points -- investigate whether they represent data errors, genuine outliers, or valid extreme values.
Try this
  1. With DFITS selected and Flagged Only labels, identify the most influential countries. China, Oman, and Myanmar typically appear as top influencers.
  2. Switch to DFBETAS (growth). Different countries may emerge -- these specifically affect the growth coefficient, not just predictions.
  3. Turn on All Countries labels and hover over points near the threshold. These borderline cases also warrant attention.

Key Concept 16.6: The democracy-growth example demonstrates omitted variable bias: the growth coefficient falls from 0.131 (bivariate) to 0.047 (with controls), a 64% reduction. Institutional variables (religion, executive constraints) were correlated with both democracy and growth, biasing the bivariate estimate upward. Always ask: "What variables might affect my outcome and correlate with my key regressor?"
Try this
  1. Start at None (bivariate): growth coef = 0.131. Add + Institutional controls (constraint + independence) -- the coefficient drops substantially.
  2. Add + Religion controls. Each set of controls absorbs some of the growth coefficient. With All Controls, it falls to 0.047.
  3. Switch to Coefficient Bar view to see the dramatic shrinkage visually. The bivariate estimate overstated the democracy-growth relationship by 64%.
