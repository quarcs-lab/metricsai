Key Concept 8.4: Outlier detection and influence. A few extreme observations can dramatically alter regression results. Always (1) identify outliers visually, (2) assess their influence on coefficients, (3) test robustness to exclusion, and (4) interpret in context.
Try this
  1. Start at 0 drops. Note the initial R² (~0.60). Slide to 1 — which country was dropped? Check the pink markers for the excluded points.
  2. Slide to 2. The USA and Luxembourg are now excluded (the two biggest residuals). R² should jump to ~0.93.
  3. Keep sliding to 5 or 6. At what point does dropping more countries stop improving R²? This reveals the "natural" scatter in the data vs. true outlier influence.

CAPM: Stock Betas

The Capital Asset Pricing Model relates stock excess returns to market excess returns. The slope (beta) measures systematic risk. Compare Coca-Cola, Target, and Walmart — which is the most defensive? Which amplifies market moves?

Key Concept 8.5: Systematic risk (beta) measures how returns co-move with the market. Beta < 1 = defensive stock (Coca-Cola, Walmart); beta > 1 = aggressive stock (Target). Only systematic risk is priced in efficient markets — idiosyncratic risk diversifies away.
Try this
  1. Start with Coca-Cola (beta ≈ 0.61). Toggle the 45° line on — the fitted line is flatter, confirming beta < 1.
  2. Switch to Target. Its beta is above 1.0 — it amplifies market movements. The scatter is wider too (lower R²).
  3. Compare Walmart — another consumer staple like Coca-Cola. Is its beta also low? Stable demand for everyday goods makes these stocks defensive.

Key Concept 8.6: R² in CAPM measures the fraction of return variation explained by market movements (systematic risk). The remaining 1 − R² is idiosyncratic risk — firm-specific shocks that diversify away in portfolios and earn no risk premium.
Try this
  1. View Residuals vs. Fitted for Coca-Cola. The cloud should look random — no fan shape or curve. This suggests homoskedasticity holds approximately.
  2. Switch to Residual Histogram. Is the distribution roughly bell-shaped? Large outliers represent months with unusual firm-specific events.
  3. Compare residual SD across all three stocks. Which stock has the most idiosyncratic risk (largest SD)? That stock benefits most from diversification.

Okun's Law: GDP Growth vs. Unemployment

Okun's Law predicts that each 1-point rise in unemployment reduces GDP growth by ~2 points. Our estimate is -1.59. Use the prediction slider to see what the model forecasts for different unemployment changes, and toggle the hypothesis reference line.

Key Concept 8.7: Okun's Law is a remarkably stable empirical regularity. Our slope of -1.59 is close to, but statistically different from, Okun's original -2.0. The relationship holds across time periods and countries, but structural changes (labor markets, technology) shift the exact coefficient.
Try this
  1. Set the prediction slider to +3 (unemployment rises 3 points). The predicted GDP growth drops sharply — this simulates a severe recession.
  2. Toggle Okun's original on. The dashed reference line is steeper (slope = -2.0). Our estimate of -1.59 suggests a somewhat weaker modern relationship.
  3. Hover over points in the lower-right. These are recession years (2008-2009, 1982) — large unemployment increases with negative GDP growth.

Key Concept 8.8: Structural breaks in time series relationships. The Okun relationship tracked well from 1961 to 2007, but the post-2008 "jobless recovery" produced systematic prediction errors. Visual inspection of actual vs. predicted over time reveals when models break down.
Try this
  1. Set the range to 1961–2007 (pre-crisis). Note the subperiod R². Now extend to 1961–2019 — does the fit change?
  2. Set 2008–2019 only. The slope and R² for this post-crisis period differ from the full sample. This is a potential structural break.
  3. Toggle to Residuals. Are the errors randomly scattered, or do you see runs of positive/negative values? Persistent patterns suggest serial correlation or structural change.
